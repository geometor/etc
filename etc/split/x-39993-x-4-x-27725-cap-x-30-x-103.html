In the plane of a triangle ABC, suppose that P = p : q : r and U = u : v : w are distinct points such that p*q*r*u*v*w is not zero, and that the point

(b-c)*p : (c-a)*q : (a-b)*r is on the line at infinity; i.e.,

(b-c)p(a,b,c) + (c-a)p(b,c,a) + (a-b)p(c,a,b) = 0.

Let CC(P,U) denote the cubic given by

(b + c)*p*(y*v - z*w)*(x*u + y*v)*(x*u + z*w) + (c + a)*q*(z*w - x*u)*(y*v + z*w)*(y*v + x*u) + (a + b)*r*(x*u - y*v)*(z*w + x*u)*(z*w + y*v) = 0.

It is easy to verify that the following twelve points lie on CC(P,U):

A, B, C; the vertices of the anticevian triangle of U; 1/u : 1/v : 1/w; a/u : b/v : c/w; b*c/u : c*a/v : a*b/w, and the vertices of the cevian triangle of the point U' shown below.

Note that the first nine points are invariant of P, but that for fixed U, distinct points P yield distinct cubics CC(P,U). Thus, for fixed U, these cubics provide examples of "Cayley's Paradox" (see Cramer's Theorem or When Nine Points Are Worth But Eight: Euler's Resolution of Cramer's Paradox.

The cubic CC(P,U) is the cubic pK(P',U'), where

P' = v^2w^2 : w^2 u^2 : u^2 v^2 and U' = v*w*(b+c)*p - (a+c)*q - (a+b)*r : :

For a discussion of pK cubics, see Special Isocubics in the Triangle Plane..
